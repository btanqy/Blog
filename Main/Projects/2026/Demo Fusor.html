Demo Fusor

In my senior year of being an undergrad, I decided that it was time for a purely for-fun project. I present the demo fusor!



Background

The purpose of a demo fusor is in the name: to demonstrate nuclear fusion. Nuclear fusion occurs when the nuclei of two atoms fuse to form a heavier atom while simultaneously releasing a huge amount of energy through radiation, mostly through neutrons . In the equation below, two deuterium fuse to form helium, a neutron, and energy.



In the demo fusor, fusion occurs in a plasma - a cloud of ionized gas with net zero charge. A roughly 10kV DC voltage is applied, where electrons are free to travel from the negatively biased center electrode to the chamber walls. During this travel, a higher concentration of electrons near the electrode collide with other atoms in the air, ionizing them and creating an avalanche of similar behavior to create a plasma. A plasma is sustained by tuning the pressure and voltage such that the mean free path (distance a electron hits before energizes another) is just right. If the mean free path is too high, energy is lost over the distance travelled and doesn't have enough energy to ionize another atom. If the mean free path is too low, electrons might not accelerate fast enough to gain enough energy for energizing another electron. A balance can be acheived by changing the electrode configuration, vacuum pressure, and electrode voltage. The Paschen's curve below describes some basic information on how to "strike" a plasma by exceeding the breakdown voltage.



The triple product describes a plasma by density, temperature, and confinement time (how long energy stays in plasma before exiting) of the ions. Another way to think of temperature is just trying to increase the thermal energy of the electrons. When the triple product exceeds a threshold called the Lawson criterion, self-sustaining fusion is possible. The lowest Lawson criterion involves fusing Deuterium and Tritium (hydrogen isotopes), which produce Helium, a neutron, and 17.6 MeV. However, for an amateur fusor, only regular air will be used. Here is the Lawson criterion for different reactions.



In more commercial and academic settings, fusion is achieved through tokamaks (left) and stellarators (right). Both utilize magnetic fields to increase the density of the plasma. These are also characterized by the scientific gain Q, which is the ratio of output energy to energy used to heat and confine the fuel. For the demo fusor, the gain can never surpass 1 because the collisions with the center electrode are lost to heat.



Another exciting method of fusion utilizes high power pulsed lasers (e.g. Nd:glass phosphate) by directing beams toward a highly pressurized point of fuel omnidirectionally. In 2013, the NIF (National Ignition Facility) was the first to make fusion net power positive by using lasers. As of April 2025, the NIF was able to achieve a Q of 4.13 for a yield of 8.6 MJ. Because of this, there are currently many startups exploring fusion energy, with one promising one using Krypton Fluoride lasers.

Building the Fusor

The primary focus in building the demo fusor is the vacuum chamber. Generally, a two-stage vacuum pump should be used to create pressures low enough to sustain vacuum at medium voltage. I only have access to a single stage pump that theoretically should pump down to 40 micron though. Here is the schematic for building the chamber.



The design uses a sanitary sight glass commonly used for viewing beer. Two flanges (or caps) are fixed to the sight glass using tri-clamps with o-rings and a thin layer of vacuum grease between them.



The two flanges are both drilled out - the top is fitted with the cross, and the bottom with the sparkplug. Both cross and sparkplug were later welded to each lid. In order to feed a high DC voltage to the grid, a sparkplug has alumina ceramic with high voltage ratings to prevent arcing.

The next step was to pot the diodes and sheath (to prevent arcing from spark plug to chamber) to prevent arcing. Although we did technically have some decent spacing (roughly 10mm) between each diode, we still wanted to pot it to be safe. I ended up getting this cheap, silicone epoxy from Amazon that semed like it would do the job. It didn't have any ratings, and I was assuming it would just work. Here is the disaster of a setup for potting each. Notice the vacuum chamber - it is vital that the epoxy gets degassed in the chamberso no air bubbles will be trapped after mixing both parts. Although this part shouldn't have been hard, there were some unnecessary difficulties with the diode enclosure not being sealed fully before potting... note to self, seal enclosures with superglue before attempting more potting.



And here are the before and afters of both the sheath and diodes:




For the center grid electrode, a material had to be chosen that would be able to withstand the thermal energy (some electrodes can glow red hot and oxidize after a while) whilst not being impossible to work with. This decision ended up being stainless steel wire. However, stainless steel wire is not the easiest to work with - normal soldering won't work since the stainless steel won't alloy to it, and welding will just melt the wire completely. The solution was brazing, which involves using high heat to wet silver solder onto the wire.



This took a lot of tries to get down right. The borax needs proper heat, or else the silver will melt off before the wire gets hot. Additionally, excessive heat can cause the borax to harden into a glassy substance, which makes it difficult for silver to come into contact with the wire. Afterwards, the oxides of the grid needed to be ground off, which was difficult with sandpaper and dremel. I found out that putting the grid in a vinegar solution helped soften the oxide, and that helped a lot with manually grinding it off later. Here's the grid sitting in vinegar solution:



I thought about possible ways to make the demo fusor modular (able to swap between electrodes, shapes, etc) without having issues with creepage. Unfortunately, there ended up significant issues with creepage as shown later, but I'm very happy with how modular and easy it is to swap out electrodes! I brazed on an M5 screw directly to the tip of the spark plug, then poured in epoxy between the spark plug, lid, and screw to keep arcing to a "minimum". Then, I could screw in a potted sheath, which includes a stainless steel wire with M5 heatsinks brazed on each side, placed in a 3D printed and potting-filled sheath. This would allow the center electrode to be screwed onto the sheath.

Testing the Fusor

I was pretty concerned about arcing in the chamber, so I wrapped up both the bottom and top of the sheath with some kapton tape. Turns out, we still had some arcing action...



After adding additional layers of kapton and ensuring the adhesive was clinging more tightly, the plasma started to form. However, there were issues with grid size, how uncentered the grid was, and the vacuum pressure. The most concerning was the vacuum pressure. In a demo fusor, we expect to see a sudden turning-on behavior, almost like a light bulb, which only happens when the electric field is high enough to ignite a sizable plasma. However, our plasma was not only weak, but had a gradual turn on, indicating that there were some significant leaks within our chamber. This first video is after inserting the large grid into the fusor.



And here is the plasma using the small grid.



Here are some more closeup images of how the poor vacuum allowed the plasma to arc only from a small portion of the grid. You can clearly see the path the electrons take that generate the plasma.




In order to chase down these vacuum leaks, I ended up practically reassembling the entire chamber. I reapplied a single layer of teflon, this time against the thread so screwing in the NPT parts would push in the teflon. I also torqued each NPT part as tight as possible, possibly destructively. In addition, I bought more silicone o-rings since clamping down the old o-rings stretched them out slightly, causing them to sit proud against the o-ring divot, which I thought was a potential vacuum leak too. I also applied a thinner layer of vacuum grease to prevent outgassing - a phenomenon where air particles travel through a certain material. Porous materials are very susceptible to outgassing, which is why JBWeld and other non-vacuum rated epoxies are difficult for keeping a vacuum. Although vacuum grease can handle better than other sealants around the o-rings, an excessive amount of vacuum grease can cause significant outgassing. Lastly, I applied some thin vacuum grease around the welding and potting, mostly for my own sanity just in case there were some air gaps.

And here is the final result, after these modifications:





There are still significant improvements needed with vacuum leaks (you can see the plasma cloud above the electrode, rather than focused at the electrode). The highest voltage pushed was just up to 10kV. The plasma clearly doesn't have enough energy to create nuclear fusion, otherwise we would see a significantly brighter and concentrated glow at the center of the electrode. However, the fusor did have enough energy to cause small amounts of wire to heat up and spark off from the plasma!
