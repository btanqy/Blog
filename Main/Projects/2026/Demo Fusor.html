Demo Fusor

Since I'm in my senior year of being an undergrad, I decided this year would be the year I have a for-fun project. I present the demo fusor!

Background

The purpose of a demo fusor is in the name: to demonstrate nuclear fusion. Nuclear fusion occurs when the nuclei of two atoms fuse to form a heavier atom while simultaneously releasing a huge amount of energy through radiating neutrons. In the equation below, two deuterium fuse to form helium, a neutron, and energy.



In the demo fusor, fusion occurs in a plasma - a cloud of ionized gas with net zero charge. A roughly 10kV DC voltage generates the plasma, where electrons are free to travel from the negatively biased center electrode to the chamber walls. A plasma is sustained by tuning the pressure and voltage such that the mean free path (distance a particle hits before hitting another). If the mean free path is too high, energy is lost over the distance travelled and doesn't have enough energy to ionize another particle. If the mean free path is too low, particles might not accelerate fast enough to gain enough energy. A balance can be acheived by changing the electrode configuration, vacuum pressure, and electrode voltage.



The triple produce is a characterization of fusion described by density, temperature, and confinement time (how long energy stays in plasma before exiting) of the ions. The lowest triple product involves fusing Deuterium and Tritium (hydrogen isotopes), which produce Helium, a neutron, and 17.6 MeV. However, for an amateur fusor, only regular air will be used.

In more commercial and academic settings, fusion is achieved through tokamaks (left) and stellarators (right). Both utilize high-energy magnetic fields to increase the density of plasma. These are also characterized by the scientific gain Q, which is the ratio of output energy to energy used to heat and confine the fuel. For the demo fusor, the gain is very low and can never surpass 1 because the ions collide with the center electrode and dissipate heat.



Another exciting method of fusion utilizes high power pulsed lasers (e.g. Nd:glass phosphate) by directing beams toward a highly pressurized point of fuel omnidirectionally. In 2013, the NIF (National Ignition Facility) was the first to make fusion net power positive by using lasers. As of April 2025, the NIF was able to achieve a Q of 4.13 for a yield of 8.6 MJ. Because of this, there are currently many startups exploring fusion energy, with one promising variation using Krypton Fluoride lasers.

Building the Fusor

The primary focus in building the demo fusor is the vacuum chamber. Generally, a two-stage vacuum pump should be used to create pressures low enough to sustain vacuum at medium voltage. I only have access to a single stage pump that theoretically should pump down to 40 micron. The design uses a sanitary sight glass commonly used for viewing beer, pictured next to the variac.



Two flanges (or caps) are fixed to the sight glass using tri-clamps with o-rings and a thin layer of vacuum grease between them.



The two flanges are both drilled out - the top is fitted with the cross, and the bottom with the sparkplug. Both cross and sparkplug were later welded to each lid. In order to feed a high DC voltage to the grid, a sparkplug has alumina ceramic with high voltage ratings to prevent arcing.

Using the lathe and an initial, pilot bit, we quickly realized this steel was taking material off the bit just as fast as the flange itself. For the center grid electrode, a material had to be chosen that would be able to withstand the thermal energy (some electrodes can glow red hot and oxidize after a while) whilst not being impossible to work with. This decision ended up being stainless steel wire. However, stainless steel wire is not the easiest to work with - normal soldering won't work since the stainless steel won't alloy to it, and welding will just melt the wire completely. The solution was brazing, which involves using high heat to wet silver solder onto the wire.


This took a lot of tries to get down right. The borax needs proper heat, or else the silver will melt off before the wire gets hot. Additionally, excessive heat can cause the borax to harden into a glassy substance, which makes it difficult for silver to come into contact with the wire. Afterwards, the oxides of the grid needed to be ground off, which was difficult with sandpaper and dremel. I found out that putting the grid in a vinegar solution helped soften the oxide, and that helped a lot with manually grinding it off later. Here's the grid sitting in vinegar solution:
