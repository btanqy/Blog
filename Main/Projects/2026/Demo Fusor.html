Background

The purpose of a demo fusor is in the name: to demonstrate nuclear fusion. Nuclear fusion occurs when the nuclei of two atoms fuse to form a heavier atom while simultaneously releasing a huge amount of energy from emitting neutrons.

Fusion occurs in a plasma - a cloud of ionized gas with net zero charge. In the demo fusor, a very high DC voltage generates the plasma by conduction of electrons through air. The demo fusor leverages this electron conduction by directing electron flow through a negatively biased, circular grid towards the grounded chamber walls.

A reference image will be inserted here in the future.

In the generated plasma, an equal amount of air atoms will have positive charge, which also experience electrostatic forces. High voltage accelerates the ions through the negatively biased grid and low enough pressure limits scattering (reduces acceleration). Plasma is sensitive to pressure by controlling the amount of collisions between air atoms. Too little pressure, and the rate of collisions between atoms will decrease and extinguish the plasma. Too high pressure, and energy is lost to collisions before plasma can be sustained. However, given enough thermal energy as well, the nuclear force overcomes electrostatic force and allows the fusion to occur.

The triple produce is a characterization of fusion described by density, temperature, and confinement time (how long energy stays in plasma before exiting) of the ions. Therefore, the lowest triple product and most widely used/practical fusion reaction (D-T) involves fusing Deuterium and Tritium (hydrogen isotopes), which produce Helium and an extra neutron. However, for an amateur fusor, only regular air will be used.

In more commercial and academic settings, fusion is achieved through tokamaks and stellarators. Both utilize high-energy magnetic fields to increase the density of plasma, which increases rate of fusion.

Building the Fusor

The primary focus of building the demo fusor is the vacuum chamber. Generally, a two-stage vacuum pump should be used to create pressures low enough to sustain vacuum at medium voltage. In anticipation of having a single stage vacuum pump, I took care to (and to be honest, I mean overcomplicated the) design of the vacuum chamber to have minimal air leaks. The design uses a sanitary sight glass commonly used for viewing beer, pictured next to the variac.



Two flanges (or caps) are fixed to the sight glass using tri-clamps with o-rings and vacuum grease between them.

A picture of the assembly in the future.

The two flanges both have feedthroughs - the top with the cross, and the bottom with the sparkplug. In order to draw, measure, and release vacuum, a hole is drilled and welded to the middle of the cross. In order to feed a high DC voltage to the grid, a sparkplug has alumina ceramic with high voltage ratings to prevent arcing.



Drilling was an absolute nightmare. The machine shop at school was very skeptical of drilling out the flanges because they were made out of 304 steel. Using the lathe and an initial, pilot bit, we quickly realized this steel was taking material off the bit just as fast as the flange itself. Not to mention the unbearable noise it made while drilling...
